Section 01

Definition of Law of Demand

"

Other things remaining equal (ceteris paribus), there exists an inverse relationship between the price of a commodity and the quantity demanded — when price rises, quantity demanded falls, and when price falls, quantity demanded rises.

— Standard NCERT / Class 11 Economics Definition
🍎 Real-Life Example

Think of mangoes. When the price of mangoes is ₹20/kg, you buy 5 kg. When the price rises to ₹80/kg, you cut back to 1 kg. Your desire for mangoes is the same — but at a higher price, you demand less. This is the Law of Demand in action.

01

Inverse Relationship

Price ↑ → Quantity Demanded ↓  |  Price ↓ → Quantity Demanded ↑

02

Ceteris Paribus Assumption

"Other things equal" — income, tastes, prices of related goods, expectations — all held constant. Only price changes.

03

It is a Law, Not an Absolute Rule

It holds true in most cases but has notable exceptions (Giffen goods, Veblen goods, etc.).

Section 02

Point-by-Point Explanation

A

Demand vs. Desire

Demand is not just a wish. It requires: (1) a desire to buy, (2) ability (income) to pay, and (3) willingness to pay. Without all three, there is no demand.

B

Demand is Always at a Price

Demand is always stated with reference to a price and a time period. "Demand for 5 units" is incomplete. "Demand for 5 units at ₹10 per unit" is correct.

C

Individual vs. Market Demand

Individual Demand: One consumer's demand at various prices. Market Demand: Sum of all individual demands in the market at each price level.

D

Movement Along vs. Shift of Demand Curve

Change in quantity demanded (due to price change) = movement along the curve. Change in demand (due to income, taste, etc.) = shift of the entire curve.

The Demand Curve

Section 05

Why Does the Law of Demand Hold? (Reasons)

01

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility (DMU)

As a consumer buys more units, the satisfaction (utility) from each additional unit falls. A consumer will buy an extra unit only if the price falls to match the lower utility. Hence, more is demanded only at lower prices.

02

Income Effect

When price falls, the real purchasing power of the consumer increases (same money buys more). This rise in real income leads to an increase in demand for the good.

03

Substitution Effect

When price of a good falls, it becomes relatively cheaper compared to its substitutes. Consumers substitute the cheaper good for the costlier one, increasing its demand.

04

New Consumers Enter the Market

At a high price, only rich consumers can afford a good. When the price drops, more consumers (with lower incomes) can now afford it — this increases total market demand.

05

Multiple Uses of a Good

Many goods have several uses (e.g., milk for drinking, making curd, ghee, etc.). At a high price, a consumer uses it for priority needs only. At a lower price, they use it for all purposes, increasing demand.

Section 06

Exceptions to the Law of Demand

In these cases, the demand curve slopes upward — more is demanded at higher prices.

1. Giffen Goods

Inferior goods on which poor people spend a large portion of their income (e.g., jowar, bajra). When price rises, their real income falls so much that they can't afford the superior substitute and buy more of the inferior good. Named after Sir Robert Giffen.

2. Veblen Goods (Goods of Ostentation / Prestige Goods)

Luxury/status goods (e.g., designer watches, luxury cars). Higher price = higher prestige. Consumers demand more because they want to display wealth. Named after Thorstein Veblen.

3. Expectation of Future Price Rise

If consumers expect prices to rise further in the future, they may buy more now even as prices are already rising (e.g., speculative buying of gold, real estate).

4. Necessities (Basic Needs)

For essential goods like life-saving medicines, consumers continue to buy even at very high prices because demand is perfectly inelastic — the law doesn't hold well here.

5. Ignorance Effect

Sometimes, consumers use price as a signal of quality. A higher-priced product is assumed to be better, so demand rises with price (e.g., branded medicine vs. generic).

Section 07

Practice Questions (Difficult)

These questions test deep understanding — including application, analysis, and HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills).

Q. 01
Hard

"The Law of Demand is based on the law of diminishing marginal utility." Critically examine this statement with reference to income and substitution effects.

💡 Hint: Explain how DMU, income effect, and substitution effect together justify the inverse price-demand relationship.
The Law of Demand is largely, but not entirely, derived from DMU. When price falls, the marginal utility of money (MU of ₹1) rises in comparison to the MU of the good, so the consumer buys more until MU again equals price. However, DMU alone is insufficient for all goods — which is why we supplement it with:

Income Effect: A fall in price increases the consumer's real income, allowing more purchases.
Substitution Effect: A fall in price makes the good relatively cheaper — consumers substitute it for pricier alternatives.

Both effects work in the same direction for normal goods, making the demand curve slope downward. For inferior goods, the income effect works in the opposite direction — which is why Giffen goods are exceptions.
Q. 02
HOTS

The price of petrol rises sharply, yet its demand does not fall significantly. Does this violate the Law of Demand? Explain the role of elasticity of demand in your answer.

💡 Hint: Think about inelastic demand and necessity goods.
No, this does NOT violate the Law of Demand. The law only states that quantity demanded will fall when price rises — it does not say by how much. Petrol is a necessity with few substitutes, so demand falls very slightly when price rises — the demand is price inelastic. The inverse relationship still exists; it's just weak. Elasticity of demand measures the degree of responsiveness, not whether the law operates.
Q. 03
Hard

During a stock market boom, people demand more shares even as their prices rise. Is this an exception to the Law of Demand? Justify your answer with economic reasoning.

💡 Hint: Think about expectations and speculative demand.
Yes, this is an apparent exception to the Law of Demand, falling under the category of "expectation of future price rise." During a boom, investors expect share prices to rise even further. They are not buying shares for current utility but for future profit. This is speculative demand — the consumer is not responding to current price but to future expected price. The ceteris paribus condition is violated here because expectations (a determinant of demand) are changing — so technically the entire demand curve has shifted rightward, not a movement along it.
Q. 04
HOTS

Distinguish between 'Change in Demand' and 'Change in Quantity Demanded' using diagrams. Explain which one is caused by the Law of Demand.

💡 Hint: One causes a movement along the curve; the other causes a shift of the curve.
Change in Quantity Demanded: Caused by a change in the own price of the good. Represented by a movement along the same demand curve — upward (contraction) when price rises, downward (expansion) when price falls. This is directly governed by the Law of Demand.

Change in Demand: Caused by changes in other determinants — income, prices of related goods, tastes, expectations. Represented by a shift of the entire demand curve — rightward (increase in demand) or leftward (decrease in demand). The Law of Demand does not govern this; it relates to non-price factors.
Q. 05
Hard

A consumer's income rises. Using the concept of normal and inferior goods, explain how this affects the position of the demand curve for each type of good. Does the Law of Demand still hold?

Normal Goods: When income rises, demand for normal goods increases — the demand curve shifts rightward. At every price level, the consumer now demands more. The Law of Demand still holds because the inverse price-demand relationship is preserved along the new curve.

Inferior Goods: When income rises, demand falls (consumers switch to superior alternatives) — the demand curve shifts leftward. Again, the Law of Demand still holds because along the new (shifted) curve, the inverse price-demand relationship is preserved. The shift is due to income (non-price factor) — it does not violate the law, it is a change in demand, not a change of the law.
Q. 06
HOTS

"The Law of Demand applies only under the condition of ceteris paribus, which is unrealistic." Do you agree? How do economists justify using this assumption?

Partially agree. The ceteris paribus assumption is indeed an abstraction — in real life, income, tastes, prices of substitutes, etc. constantly change. However, economists use this assumption as a scientific method to isolate and study the pure effect of one variable (price) on another (quantity demanded). It is similar to how physics experiments control all variables except one. Without this, it would be impossible to establish any reliable economic law. The assumption is not about describing reality perfectly — it's a methodological tool to analyze partial relationships. In practice, economists then relax the assumption to study fuller, real-world scenarios.
